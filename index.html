Introduction to Forces

A force is a push or pull that acts on an object and requires a specific agent or cause to exert it. A force is a vector quantity, which means it has both magnitude and direction. The two types of forces are contact forces and long range forces. Contact forces are forces that need physical touch between objects at a specific point while long-range forces are forces that act on an object without physical contact. Friction, tension, and normal force are contact forces. Weight, magnetic, and electrostatic are examples of long-range forces.

Types of Forces

Weight Force (F_w)

Weight force is caused by gravity. It always pulls straight down, no matter what the object is doing. The symbol for weight is F_w or F_mg, and the agent is the Earth pulling on the object. Example: When you’re standing still or jumping, your weight is always pulling you toward the ground. Even when you're flying in a plane or riding a roller coaster, the weight force still pulls downward as it’s constant and always present because gravity never stops acting.

Spring Force (F_s)

Spring force happens when a material tries to return to its original shape after being stretched or compressed. Springs pull when stretched and push when squeezed. The force always acts along the direction of the spring. Example: When you jump on a trampoline, the springs stretch and then push you back up. That upward force is the spring force trying to return the trampoline to its resting shape.

Normal Force (N⃗)

Normal force is the force a surface applies to support an object resting on it. It acts perpendicular or “normal” to the surface. Example: When you sit in a chair, the chair pushes up on you to support your weight. That upward push is the normal force. Without it, you would fall through. If you place a book on a tilted desk, the normal force points perpendicular to the surface and not straight up.

Tension Force (F_t)

Tension force is the pulling force from something like a rope, cable, or string. Tension is always directed along the line of the rope or string. Example: When someone is holding the end of a rope and pulling a sled, the rope pulls the sled forward. The rope becomes tight and transmits a tension force from the person’s hand to the sled.

Friction Force (F_k for kinetic, F_s for static)

Friction is a force that resists sliding between two surfaces. It opposes the motion of one surface against another that its in constant with. Static friction keeps things from starting to move, and kinetic friction acts while things are already sliding. You only need to worry about friction if the problem mentions it. If the surface is rough or gives a friction number, you must include friction. Example: When you try to push a heavy couch and it won’t move right away, that’s static friction resisting the motion. Once it starts sliding, kinetic friction keeps pushing back against your motion and makes it harder to keep moving.

Drag Force (F_d)

Drag force is the resistance an object encounters when moving through fluids like water and air. It always acts in the opposite direction of the object's movement. When solving a force problem, assume there is no air resistance unless it is stated. Example: When a car speeds up, the air pushes back against it and slows it down. That resistance is drag.

Thrust Force (F_thrust)

Thrust is a force that propels an object forward by accelerating or generating a mass in the opposite direction. It’s often created by engines or propellers. Example: When a rocket launches, it pushes gas downward and the reaction force pushes the rocket upward. Airplanes also use thrust from their engines to move forward and overcome air resistance.

Magnetic Force (F_m)

Magnetic force is a force that can attract or repel certain materials, like iron, and acts without contact. It also affects moving electric charges. Example: When you bring a magnet near paperclips, they snap toward it even if there’s a small gap in between. That pulling force is magnetic force acting across empty space.

Electric Force (F_e)

Electric force is a push or pull between charged objects. Opposite charges attract, and like charges repel. It works without the objects needing to touch. Example: If you rub a balloon on your hair, it builds up electric charge and sticks to the wall. The force between the charged balloon and the neutral wall pulls the balloon in, even without contact.

Newton’s Laws

Newton’s First Law

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion until an unbalanced force acts on it. An unbalanced force is a force on the object that is not zero and causes the object to change its motion. Example: A soccer ball will sit still on the field until someone kicks it. Once kicked, it keeps rolling until forces like friction and gravity slow it down and eventually stop its movement.

Newton’s Second Law

Newton’s second law tells us how forces affect the motion of objects. It says that when a net force acts on an object, the object will accelerate. The acceleration depends on two things. It depends on the force applied, where a stronger force produces more acceleration as they are directly proportional. It also depends on the mass of the object, where a heavier mass results in less acceleration as they are inversely proportional. This relationship is expressed by the formula a = F/m or F=ma

F is the net force measured in newtons (N). It is the vector sum of all forces acting on the object. m is the objects mass. a is the objects acceleration.

Example: Pushing an empty shopping cart makes it speed up more than a full one. This is because the same force causes less acceleration when the mass is larger (F=ma)

Newton’s Third Law

The third law describes how forces work when two objects interact with each other. Whenever one object exerts a force on another, the second object exerts an equal force back in the opposite direction.

The forces always come as a pair, called an action-reaction pair.

1. They are equal in size. 2. They point in opposite directions. 3. They act on two different objects.

Example: When you jump off a small boat, the boat moves backward. This is because when you push the boat one way, the boat pushes you equally in the opposite direction.

Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram is a simplified drawing that shows all the forces acting on a single object.

1. Isolate the object you want to analyze by representing it as a particle

2. Identify all the forces acting on the object.

3. Draw vectors to represent each force. Start each tail from the particle and point it in the direction the force acts.

4. Label each force clearly by using their standard symbols, such as F_N for normal force. The labels do not include numbers.

Problem 1:

(b) Determine the horizontal acceleration of the block.
